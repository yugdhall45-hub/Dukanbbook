Chapter 01

What is Gravity?

From a falling apple to the bending of spacetime — gravity is the universe's most familiar yet mysterious force.

1687

Newton's Law of Gravitation

In 1687, Sir Isaac Newton published his Principia Mathematica, introducing the Law of Universal Gravitation: every particle of matter in the universe attracts every other particle with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r²
F = gravitational force G = gravitational constant (6.674×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²) m₁, m₂ = masses of objects r = distance between centers

This equation explained why planets orbit the Sun, why the Moon orbits Earth, and why objects fall to the ground — all with remarkable precision. It dominated physics for over 200 years.

1915

Einstein's General Relativity

In 1915, Albert Einstein revolutionized our understanding with General Relativity. Rather than a force, Einstein described gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.

"Spacetime tells matter how to move; matter tells spacetime how to curve."
— John Archibald Wheeler

Imagine a stretched rubber sheet. Place a heavy ball on it and it creates a well — other balls roll toward it. That well is gravity. Massive objects like stars and black holes warp the fabric of spacetime, and objects follow the curved paths — what we perceive as being "pulled."

🌀
Gravitational Time Dilation

Time passes slower in stronger gravitational fields — verified by GPS satellites.

💡
Light Bending

Light follows curved spacetime. During a 1919 eclipse, Eddington confirmed this.

🌊
Gravitational Waves

Ripples in spacetime from massive events — detected by LIGO in 2016.

Effects

How Gravity Shapes Everything

From atoms to galaxy clusters, gravity is the architect of cosmic structure.

🌍

Planetary Formation

Gravity pulls gas and dust together, forming planetesimals that collide and grow into planets over millions of years. Without gravity, worlds simply wouldn't exist.

🌙

Tidal Forces

The Moon's gravity creates Earth's ocean tides by pulling differently on near and far sides. These tidal forces also drive geological activity on Jupiter's moon Io.

⚫

Black Holes

When a massive star collapses, gravity becomes so extreme that even light cannot escape. The boundary of no return is called the event horizon.

✨

Stellar Fusion

Stars exist because gravity compresses hydrogen gas until it ignites nuclear fusion, releasing the light and heat that powers all life on Earth.

🌌

Galaxy Structure

Galaxies are vast gravitational systems — hundreds of billions of stars bound together. Even dark matter influences galactic rotation through its gravity.

🔭

Gravitational Lensing

Massive objects bend light from distant sources, acting as natural telescopes. Astronomers use this effect to detect planets and dark matter.
